Why this matters

Every system administrator eventually steps away from the terminal and toward the hardware — reseating a card, terminating a cable run, or diagnosing a dead power supply. These three sessions walk through the manual tools, wiring practices, and component-testing skills that make that kind of hands-on troubleshooting safe and reliable.

Section One

Use Hand Tools

Presented by Adrian Apellanes

Hand tools are the most basic, most frequently used equipment in electronics and electrical work. Unlike power tools, they run on manual effort alone — reliable, portable, and essential for everyday IT tasks like assembling computers, repairing devices, and wiring simple circuits.

Common Hand Tools

1. Screwdrivers (Flathead & Phillips)

  • Tighten or loosen screws holding computer casings, motherboards, and electrical panels together.
  • Come in different sizes and tip types — flathead for slotted screws, Phillips for cross-head screws.
  • Magnetized tips help with small screws in tight spaces, like inside a laptop.
  • Proper use: match the screwdriver to the screw head to avoid stripping it; apply steady, downward pressure while turning.
  • Application: opening computer cases, replacing hard drives, mounting electrical outlets.

2. Pliers (Long-nose, Combination, Diagonal Cutting)

  • Long-nose pliers grip small components and reach into tight spaces.
  • Combination pliers grip, bend, and cut wires of moderate thickness.
  • Diagonal cutters are used specifically for cutting wires and component leads.
  • Proper use: use the correct plier type for the task; never use pliers as a hammer or to cut hardened material.
  • Application: bending component leads, holding wires during soldering, trimming excess wire after termination.

3. Wire Strippers

  • Remove plastic or rubber insulation from wires without damaging the copper conductor inside.
  • Available in manual (adjustable notch) and automatic (self-adjusting) types.
  • Proper use: select the notch that matches the wire gauge (AWG size), squeeze gently, and pull to remove insulation cleanly.
  • Application: preparing wires for splicing, terminal connections, or network cable (UTP) preparation.

4. Multimeter

  • A multi-purpose testing instrument that measures voltage (AC/DC), current, resistance, and continuity.
  • Available as analog (needle display) or digital (numeric display, more common today).
  • Proper use: set the correct measurement mode and range before testing; always confirm test leads are in the correct ports.
  • Application: checking power supply output, testing cable continuity, diagnosing faulty components.

5. Soldering Iron

  • A heated tool that melts solder, creating a permanent, low-resistance bond between components and circuit boards.
  • Typically rated between 15–60 watts depending on the application.
  • Proper use: let the iron reach temperature, tin the tip with a little solder, and heat the joint briefly before feeding solder in.
  • Application: repairing circuit boards, attaching connectors, joining wires permanently.

Other Supporting Hand Tools

Tweezers

Handle very small components without contaminating them with skin oils.

Anti-static wrist strap

Grounds the technician to prevent electrostatic discharge (ESD) damage to sensitive chips.

Crimping tool

Attaches connectors — like RJ45 — to a cable end by compressing a metal contact onto the wire.

Allen wrench (hex key)

Tightens hexagonal-socket screws found in equipment racks and enclosures.

Desoldering pump

Removes melted solder to detach components or correct a mistake — also called a "solder sucker."

⚠ Safety Tips
  • Always disconnect power before working on any electrical or electronic device.
  • Use insulated tools when working near live circuits.
  • Wear an anti-static wrist strap when handling sensitive components (motherboards, RAM, ICs).
  • Never point diagonal cutters or strippers toward yourself or others.
  • Keep the soldering iron in its stand when not in use, and never touch the heated tip.
  • Inspect tools regularly for damage — cracked handles, worn tips — and replace as needed.
  • Work in a well-ventilated area when soldering to avoid inhaling flux fumes.

Real-World Applications in IT/Electronics Maintenance

  • Assembling and upgrading desktop computers — installing RAM, drives, expansion cards.
  • Repairing power adapters, cables, and connectors.
  • Building and terminating network cables for LAN installation.
  • Performing preventive maintenance on servers and networking equipment.
  • Diagnosing hardware issues with a multimeter before replacing parts.
Summary

Hand tools form the backbone of practical electronics and IT maintenance work. Mastery of tools like screwdrivers, pliers, wire strippers, multimeters, and soldering irons — combined with strict safety habits — lets technicians repair and install with confidence and precision.

Terminate & Connect Electrical Wiring and Electronic Circuits

Presented by Vemark Apostadero

Termination is the process of properly ending or joining a wire so it can be safely and reliably connected to a device, terminal, or another wire. Poor termination is one of the leading causes of electrical failures, overheating, and equipment damage.

Common Wire Termination Methods

1. Soldering

  • Joins wires or components with melted solder, creating a strong, permanent, highly conductive bond.
  • Best suited for permanent connections on circuit boards and delicate electronic assemblies.
  • Advantages: very reliable, resistant to vibration once cooled.
  • Limitations: requires skill and heat control; not ideal for connections that need to be undone often.

2. Crimping

  • Uses a crimping tool to mechanically compress a metal connector — lug, ferrule, or terminal — onto a stripped wire end.
  • Common in automotive wiring, network cabling (RJ45 connectors), and industrial control panels.
  • Advantages: fast, no heat required, consistent results when done correctly.
  • Limitations: needs the correct connector size and crimping die; a poor crimp can loosen over time.

3. Wire Nuts (Twist-on Connectors)

  • Plastic caps with an internal spring that twist onto two or more stripped wire ends to join them.
  • Widely used in residential and commercial electrical wiring, such as junction boxes.
  • Advantages: quick, tool-free aside from stripping, reusable and easy to inspect.
  • Limitations: not suitable for high-vibration environments; must be sized correctly for the wire gauge and count.

4. Terminal Blocks

  • Modular blocks with screw or spring-clamp connections that let multiple wires connect in a fixed, organized position.
  • Common in control panels, power distribution boards, and equipment enclosures.
  • Advantages: organized, easy to modify or replace individual connections, supports labeling.
  • Limitations: screw terminals need periodic re-tightening to prevent loosening.

Comparison of Termination Methods

MethodBest Use CaseTool Required
SolderingCircuit boards, permanent electronic jointsSoldering iron, solder
CrimpingConnectors, network cables, automotive wiringCrimping tool
Wire NutsHousehold / junction box wiringWire stripper only
Terminal BlocksControl panels, distribution boardsScrewdriver

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Turn off and lock out the power source. Confirm zero voltage using a multimeter or voltage tester.
  2. Measure and cut the wire to the required length using cutting pliers.
  3. Strip the insulation from the wire end, exposing about 6–10 mm of bare copper.
  4. Select the appropriate termination method — solder, crimp connector, wire nut, or terminal block — based on the application.
  5. Join the wire: solder and let cool, crimp firmly with the correct die, twist on the wire nut until snug, or insert into the terminal block and tighten the screw.
  6. Inspect the connection for tightness, exposed copper, and proper insulation coverage.
  7. Test continuity and proper connection with a multimeter before restoring power.
  8. Restore power and verify correct operation of the circuit or device.
⚠ Safety Standards to Follow
  • Always de-energize and lock out/tag out (LOTO) circuits before working on them.
  • Match wire gauge and connector type to the current rating of the circuit.
  • Never leave exposed copper conductors uninsulated after termination.
  • Use insulating tape or heat-shrink tubing to cover soldered or spliced joints.
  • Follow the Philippine Electrical Code (PEC) or applicable local wiring standards.
  • Do not overload terminal blocks or wire nuts beyond their rated wire capacity.
  • Label wires and terminals clearly to avoid confusion during future maintenance.
Summary

Proper termination and connection — whether by soldering, crimping, wire nuts, or terminal blocks — ensures safe, reliable, long-lasting electrical and electronic connections. A consistent procedure plus strict safety standards prevents equipment damage, hazards, and costly downtime.

Test Electronic Components

Presented by Alken Jones Steven Bacsarpa

Testing electronic components is a critical diagnostic skill — it tells a technician whether a component is still good or has failed, before any part gets replaced. The multimeter is the primary tool for this, measuring resistance, voltage, and continuity, often with a dedicated diode-test mode.

Testing Common Components

1. Resistors

  • Function: limits or controls the flow of electric current in a circuit.
  • How to test: set the multimeter to resistance (Ω) mode, remove the resistor from the circuit (or at least one leg), and place the probes across its leads.
  • Working: reading is close to the rated value from its color code, within tolerance.
  • Faulty: reading is infinite (open/burned out) or far from the rated value.

2. Capacitors

  • Function: stores and releases electrical energy; smooths voltage in power supplies.
  • How to test: discharge the capacitor first, then use capacitance mode if available, or resistance mode to observe charging behavior.
  • Working: resistance reading starts low and climbs toward infinity as it charges; on capacitance mode, the value matches its rating.
  • Faulty: zero ohms (shorted), infinite with no charging (open), or a bulging/leaking case.

3. Diodes

  • Function: allows current to flow in only one direction.
  • How to test: use diode-test mode; red probe on the anode, black on the cathode, then reverse the probes.
  • Working: shows a 0.5–0.7V drop in one direction (forward bias), no reading ("OL") in reverse.
  • Faulty: a reading in both directions (shorted), or no reading in either direction (open).

4. Transistors

  • Function: acts as a switch or amplifier for electronic signals.
  • How to test: in diode-test mode, check the base-emitter and base-collector junctions like two back-to-back diodes.
  • Working: each junction shows a normal forward drop in one direction only, like a diode.
  • Faulty: a junction reads shorted (zero both ways) or open (no reading either way).

5. Fuses

  • Function: protects a circuit by breaking the connection when current exceeds a safe level.
  • How to test: continuity or resistance mode, probes on each end of the fuse.
  • Working: shows continuity — a beep and/or near-zero resistance.
  • Faulty: no continuity (infinite resistance) — the fuse has blown.

Practical Testing Procedure

  1. Power off and discharge the circuit or device before testing — capacitors especially should be safely discharged.
  2. Visually inspect the component for obvious damage: burn marks, bulging, cracks, discoloration.
  3. Desolder or lift at least one leg of the component if an isolated reading is needed.
  4. Select the correct multimeter mode — resistance, capacitance, diode test, or continuity — for the component.
  5. Connect the probes correctly, noting polarity for diodes, transistors, and polarized capacitors.
  6. Record and compare the reading against the expected or rated value.
  7. Conclude whether the component is working, degraded, or faulty, and replace as needed.
⚠ Safety Tips
  • Always discharge capacitors before testing — they can retain dangerous voltage even when unpowered.
  • Set the multimeter to the correct mode before connecting probes, to avoid damaging the meter or circuit.
  • Never test components while they're connected to a live power source.
  • Handle components by the body, not the leads, to avoid ESD damage or bent pins.
  • Double-check probe polarity on diodes and electrolytic capacitors.
  • Use a fresh multimeter battery and good-quality probes for accurate readings.
Summary

Testing electronic components with a multimeter is an essential troubleshooting skill — it lets technicians quickly identify faulty resistors, capacitors, diodes, transistors, and fuses. Recognizing the clear signs of working versus faulty parts keeps diagnosis accurate and avoids unnecessary replacements.
